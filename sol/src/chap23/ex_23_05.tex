\exercise{Paper supply}{3}
In an office the copier paper is stored in a cabinet. At an average rate of once a week an employee takes paper out of the cabinet and loads it into the copier. There is a 50\% chance that the employee tries to load only one packet of paper, and a 50\% chance that the employee tries to load two packets of paper. If the cabinet is empty, the office manager immediately orders 4 new packets of paper which arrive on average within 2 weeks. 

\subquestion Consider this system as a network of states, numbered 0 to 4. The system is in state $i$ if there are $i$ packets of paper in the cabinet. Write a system of differential equations for the variables $x_0,\ldots,x_4$,
where $x_i$ is the probability that the system is in state $i$.

\solution
The system of equations is 
\eqa{
\dot{x}_0 &=& - 0.5 x_0 + x_1 + 0.5 x_2 \\
\dot{x}_1 &=& - x_1 + 0.5 x_2 + 0.5 x_3 \\
\dot{x}_2 &=& - x_2 + 0.5 x_3 + 0.5 x_4 \\
\dot{x}_3 &=& - x_3 + 0.5 x_4 \\
\dot{x}_4 &=& - x_4 + 0.5 x_0
}

\subquestion Write the differential equation system in the form 
$\dot{\vec{x}}= -{\bf L} \vec{x}$
where $\vec{x}=(x_0,\ldots,x_4)^{\rm T}$.

\solution
We find 
\eq{
{\bf L} = \left(\begin{array}{c c c c c} 
0.5  & -1 & -0.5 & 0 & 0 \\
0    &  1 & -0.5 & -0.5 & 0 \\
0    &  0 & 1    & -0.5 & -0.5 \\
0    &  0 & 0    & 1 & -0.5 \\
-0.5 &  0 & 0    & 0 & 1 
\end{array} \right)
}

\subquestion Use Kirchhoff's theorem to compute the probability that there isn't any paper in the cabinet. (i.e.~compute $x_0$ in the steady state).

\solution 
The easiest solution is to use Kirchhoff together with the matrix-tree theorem. This yields

\eq{
S_1 = \left|\begin{array}{c c c c} 
  1 & -0.5 & -0.5 & 0 \\
  0 & 1    & -0.5 & -0.5 \\
  0 & 0    & 1 & -0.5 \\
  0 & 0    & 0 & 1 
\end{array} \right| 
=1
}
where we have used that the determinant is the product of the eigenvalues, and in this case we can see immediately that all the eigenvalues are 1 due to the upper triangular form of the matrix.

\eqa{
S_2 &=& \left|\begin{array}{c c c c} 
0.5  & -0.5 & 0 & 0 \\
0    & 1    & -0.5 & -0.5 \\
0    & 0    & 1 & -0.5 \\
-0.5 & 0    & 0 & 1 
\end{array} \right| \\
&=& 0.5 \left|\begin{array}{c c c} 
 1    & -0.5 & -0.5 \\
  0    & 1 & -0.5 \\
    0    & 0 & 1 
\end{array} \right| + 0.5
\left|\begin{array}{c c c} 
-0.5  & 0 & 0 \\
1   & -0.5    & -0.5 \\
0    & 1    & -0.5 
\end{array} \right| = 0.3125
}

\eq{
S_3 = \left|\begin{array}{c c c c} 
0.5  & -1 & 0 & 0 \\
0    &  1 & -0.5 & 0 \\
0    &  0 & 1 & -0.5 \\
-0.5 &  0 & 0 & 1 
\end{array} \right| = 0.375 \\
}
\eq{
S_4 = \left|\begin{array}{c c c c} 
0.5  & -1 & -0.5 & 0 \\
0    &  1 & -0.5 & 0 \\
0    &  0 & 1    & -0.5 \\
-0.5 &  0 & 0    & 1 
\end{array} \right| = 0.25
}
\eq{
S_5 = \left|\begin{array}{c c c c} 
0.5  & -1 & -0.5 & 0  \\
0    &  1 & -0.5 & -0.5  \\
0    &  0 & 1    & -0.5  \\
0    &  0 & 0    & 1  
\end{array} \right| = 0.5 
}
In summary we have 
\eq{
S_1=\frac{16}{16} \quad S_2=\frac{5}{16} \quad S_3=\frac{6}{16} \quad S_4=\frac{4}{16} \quad S_5=\frac{8}{16}
}
hence
\eq{
\sum S_i = \frac{39}{16}
}
so we can find the steady state
\eq{
X^*_1=\frac{16}{39} \quad X^*_2=\frac{5}{39}\quad X^*_3=\frac{6}{39}\quad X^*_4=\frac{4}{39} \quad X^*_5=\frac{8}{39}.
}
